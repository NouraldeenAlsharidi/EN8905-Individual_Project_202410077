Ethical Responsibilities & Social Dimensions
of the Energy Transition

Who benefits, who bears the cost, and what engineering ethics demands

Posted: 6 July 2026  ·  Nouraldeen M Alsharidi

Stage 4 — Ethics & Social Dimensions

Ethical Framework: IEEE Code of Ethics

As an electrical engineer, I reference the IEEE Code of Ethics — specifically the principles of holding paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public, contributing to sustainable development, and being honest in technical assessments. I also draw on the UN Engineers Without Borders ethical framework, which emphasises equity, participation, and do-no-harm.

Ethical Trade-offs in the Transition to Offshore Wind

Ethical IssueGas (Status Quo)Offshore Wind (Transition)
Climate justice Continues emissions harming vulnerable populations globally Reduces Bahrain's contribution to global warming
Energy affordability Currently subsidised; low end-user cost High CAPEX may raise electricity prices initially
Energy access equity Universal in Bahrain; but global gas scarcity affects poor nations Distributed wind can provide electricity to off-grid communities globally
Worker transition Existing gas sector jobs at risk Offshore wind creates new jobs — installation, O&M (est. 5× gas jobs/MW)
Environmental harm CO₂, NOₓ, methane; contributes to 1.5 °C breach Bird/bat impact, seabed disruption (manageable with siting)

Who Is Affected?

Locally (Bahrain)

  • Coastal communities: Climate change raises sea levels threatening Bahrain's low-lying land area; wind transition reduces this risk
  • Industrial workers (e.g. SABIC): Energy cost stability protects jobs dependent on electricity-intensive processes
  • Low-income households: Subsidy removal during transition could increase bills — policy must include protection measures
  • Fishermen: Offshore wind installations require navigation exclusion zones — consultation is essential

Globally

  • Climate-vulnerable nations (Pacific islands, Bangladesh, sub-Saharan Africa): benefit from every tonne of CO₂ avoided
  • Energy-poor populations: 733 million people lack electricity access (IEA 2024) — a global justice issue SDG 7 and 13 both address
  • Future generations: Every year of delay in transitioning increases the cost of meeting the 1.5 °C target by ~USD 1 trillion globally (McKinsey, 2022)

Community Need & Why Engagement Matters

Public acceptance is a prerequisite for offshore wind deployment. In Bahrain, fishing communities have historically opposed offshore infrastructure without adequate consultation. The ethical obligation of engineers is not just technical — it is to engage, explain, and co-design solutions with affected communities.

Equity Concern: Bahrain's per-capita electricity consumption is 9,700 kWh/yr — vs. 180 kWh/yr in sub-Saharan Africa. Gulf states have a particular moral responsibility to decarbonise rapidly, given their disproportionate historical contribution to global emissions per capita.
Ethical Conclusion: The transition to offshore wind is ethically imperative under the IEEE Code of Ethics. It requires careful management of the affordability trade-off, worker retraining, fishermen consultation, and transparent public communication — with government policy ensuring that the cost burden does not fall on the least able to pay.
