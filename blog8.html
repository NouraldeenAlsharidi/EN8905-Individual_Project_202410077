Blog Entry 8 · Final Reflection & Conclusions

Final Reflection & Conclusions:
What This Project Taught Me

Engineering judgement, personal growth, and a call to action for Bahrain

Posted: 13 July 2026  ·  Nouraldeen M Alsharidi

All Stages — Final Reflection

Summary of Findings Across All 5 Stages

StageKey Finding
Stage 1: SDG Research SDG 13 demands rapid decarbonisation of electricity. Bahrain's gas dependency is directly incompatible with the 1.5 °C Paris target. Bahrain's NREP 2021 commits to 20% renewables by 2035 — offshore wind is the most technically feasible pathway.
Stage 2: Energy Assessment Natural gas (CCGT) scores well on reliability (85–92% CF, despatchable) but scores poorly on sustainability (490 g CO₂/kWh, import dependency). Offshore wind scores well on emissions (12 g CO₂/kWh), fuel security, and long-run cost — but faces intermittency and high CAPEX challenges. Recommended: hybrid transition with BESS.
Stage 3: Practical Activity Wind power calculation confirmed 280 GWh/yr from a 10-turbine (80 MW) farm — avoiding 137,000 tCO₂/yr. SABIC ICS energy audit found 231 MWh/yr consumption; wind power would cut this department's CO₂ by 97.5%.
Stage 4: Ethics IEEE ethics mandate the transition. Key trade-offs: affordability for low-income households, job transitions for gas workers, and fishermen consultation for offshore siting. Ethically, delay is the worst option.
Stage 5: Social Awareness A small group of SABIC colleagues, 74% average quiz score. High understanding of SDG concepts; lower on engineering calculations (expected). Key outcome: awareness sparked professional interest in renewable PPAs at SABIC.

Personal Reflection — What I Learned as an Electrical Engineer

Before this project, I understood wind turbines technically — generators, power electronics, grid connection. What this project added was systems thinking: understanding that the technical choice of an energy source has cascading effects on climate, economics, social equity, and geopolitics.

Working at SABIC in the ICS department, I see every day how much electricity industrial processes consume. The 113 tonnes of CO₂ per year from just one small department — multiplied across SABIC's full site — shows that industrial decarbonisation is not abstract: it is an engineering problem I am professionally positioned to contribute to.

The social awareness activity was the most personally challenging part. Explaining engineering concepts without jargon required me to deeply understand them first — you only truly understand something when you can explain it to a non-expert. The analogy of "renting vs. buying a car" for gas vs. wind was developed in this process and proved highly effective.

Call to Action — Recommendations for Bahrain

  1. Commission a 100 MW offshore wind pilot: Shallow Gulf waters near the northern coast of Bahrain are technically suitable. A pilot would generate real performance data for the region.
  2. Introduce carbon pricing: Even a modest BD 5/tonne CO₂ signal would change the economic balance between gas and wind, accelerating private investment.
  3. Develop grid-scale BESS: To complement wind intermittency; battery storage costs have fallen 90% since 2010 (IRENA) and are now cost-competitive for 2–4 hour shifting.
  4. Worker transition programme: SABIC and EWA engineers should be retrained in offshore wind O&M — the skill sets in instrumentation, electrical, and mechanical engineering transfer directly.
  5. Consult coastal communities early: Involve fishermen and coastal residents in siting decisions from day one — not as an afterthought.
Final Conclusion: Transitioning Bahrain's electricity generation from natural gas to offshore wind, supported by BESS and CCGT peaking backup, is technically feasible, economically viable within 10 years as costs continue to fall, environmentally essential for SDG 13 compliance, and ethically imperative for climate justice. As an electrical engineering professional at SABIC, I intend to champion this transition from within industry.
