How Offshore Wind Works — Technical Overview

Offshore wind turbines convert kinetic energy in wind to electrical energy using the lift force on aerodynamic blades rotating a shaft connected to a generator. Modern offshore turbines (e.g. Siemens Gamesa SG 14-222 DD) use Permanent Magnet Synchronous Generators (PMSG) with full-power converters, enabling variable-speed operation and grid-compatible AC output at 50 Hz.

Rated Power: 14 MW per turbine. Hub height: 150 m. Rotor diameter: 222 m. A 100-turbine offshore wind farm = 1,400 MW — equivalent to one large gas plant.

Justified Judgement

Engineering Judgement: Offshore wind is the superior long-term choice for meeting Bahrain's energy needs sustainably under SDG 13. Although it has a higher current LCOE ($0.084 vs $0.068/kWh) and intermittency challenges, its near-zero lifecycle emissions (97% lower than gas), zero fuel cost exposure, and rapidly falling cost trajectory make it the only technically viable pathway to decarbonise Bahrain's grid by 2050. The recommended approach is a hybrid transition: offshore wind for baseload with CCGT kept as peaking backup, paired with battery storage (BESS), ramping to 50% wind by 2035 aligned with NREP targets.
